Guide to choosing garment bag sizes

Best for: dry cleaners, alteration shops, uniform services, retailers.
Shop garment bags

1) Measure the garment (not the hanger)

  • Width: shoulder-to-shoulder at the widest point.
  • Length: from the top of the hanger hook area to the bottom of the garment.
  • Bulk: coats and layered items need extra room (gusset / wider bag).

2) Quick sizing rules (easy to remember)

  • Bag width ≈ garment width + 4–6 inches (room for the hanger + movement).
  • Bag length ≈ garment length + 4–8 inches (so hems don’t press the bottom).
  • Gusset: use gusseted bags for bulky winter coats, layered suits, or multiple items on one hanger.

3) Common use-cases (quick reference)

Use-case Typical width Typical length Notes
Shirts / blouses 18–21" 36–40" Most daily dry-cleaning volume.
Pants (folded) 18–21" 36–40" Fold over hanger; shorter bags often work.
Suits / dresses 21–24" 40–54" Go longer for formalwear and maxi dresses.
Coats / outerwear 24–28" 48–60" Consider gusset + thicker film for transport.
Wedding / formal gowns 24–30"+ 60–72" Extra room prevents wrinkling and snagging.

4) Choose the right thickness

For daily use, “standard” thickness is often enough. If customers transport garments long distance, or items are heavy/bulky, go thicker. Use the next guide for a simple thickness decision.

Tip
If you’re between two sizes, choose the longer length first (it reduces crushed hems), then adjust width.
Guide

Heavy vs Extra Heavy: which thickness do you need?

A simple decision guide for poly film thickness (often measured in mil).
Shop poly bags

What “heavy” and “extra heavy” usually mean

  • Heavy: a common choice for everyday handling (good balance of cost + durability).
  • Extra heavy: higher puncture/tear resistance for sharper items, heavier loads, or rougher transport.

Thickness naming varies by supplier; always check the product’s stated mil / gauge if you need a precise spec.

Pick “Heavy” when

  • You’re packing lightweight items (folded garments, liners, standard packaging).
  • Orders are short-distance/local and handled gently.
  • You want the best value for high monthly volume.

Pick “Extra Heavy” when

  • Items have edges/corners (hangers in bulk, hardware, sharp boxed items).
  • Loads are heavy or stacked in transit.
  • Customers carry garments long distance or you need fewer tears/returns.
Quick test
If you’re double-bagging often, it’s usually cheaper (and cleaner) to switch to a thicker bag once.

How much packaging do dry cleaners use per month?

Use this simple method to estimate monthly bags, hangers, and wrap needs.
Get a bulk quote

Step 1: Start with garments per day

Monthly garments = (average garments/day) × (operating days/month)

Example: 160 garments/day × 26 days = 4,160 garments/month

Step 2: Add “mix” by category

Most shops have a mix (example below). Adjust to match your business.

Category Share Packaging used
Shirts / tops 45% Short garment bags
Pants / skirts 25% Short garment bags (often)
Suits / dresses 20% Medium/long garment bags
Coats / outerwear 10% Long + wider (often gusseted) bags

Step 3: Add a waste/redo buffer

  • Recommended buffer: 5–12% (mis-bags, tears, rewraps, rush orders).
  • Higher buffer if you do lots of delivery routes or high-volume weekends.

Monthly order quantity = estimated usage × (1 + buffer)

Step 4: Convert to a buying plan

  • Daily staples (most common sizes): keep 2–4 weeks of stock.
  • Specialty sizes (extra-long / gusset): keep 4–8 weeks of stock (lower volume).
  • Delivery/transport businesses: plan extra for rewraps and heavier film.
